Motivation

In 2019, women made up 44% of all sports participants, but received less than 5% of sports media coverage and less than 1% of total sports sponsorship dollars. In recent years, however, women’s sports have experienced a rapid rise in popularity. This has coincided with the growth of the Women’s National Basketball Association (WNBA). The 2025 season set new records for both attendance and viewership. The league, which currently consists of 13 teams, has announced that it plans to expand to 18 teams by 2030. The growth of the WNBA has also created new opportunities for women off the court with more women occupying positions as media commentators, analysts, coaches, and executives. However, there is still work to be done. Although the WNBA is receiving more attention, athletes aren’t being compensated at an equivalent rate. Players, uniting under slogans like “Pay Us What You Owe Us,” are demanding a larger cut of the league’s increased media revenue. As student-athletes, we were interested in exploring the 2025 WNBA season, analyzing performance at the team and individual levels and how it relates to player earnings and popularity.

Research Question

Our primary research question is: what factors were associated with individual and team success throughout the 2025 season?

In this analysis, we present insightful ways of measuring and visualizing team and player performance. We incorporate machine learning for the purposes of determining the best predictors of game outcome and validating our summaries of player performance. We also explore the relationships between player performance and between salary and social media presence.

Background

Data

Wehoop is an R package for analyzing women’s college and professional basketball data. It is a scraping and aggregating interface for ESPN’s women’s college basketball and WNBA statistics developed by Saiem Gilani and Geoff Hutchinson. In this analysis, we use wnba play-by-play, team box score, and player box score datasets from 2025. The play-by-play dataset (121,342 x 62) contains every play from every game. It has text explaining the type of play (layup, rebound, foul, substitution, free throw, etc.) and players involved. It also has timestamps, player coordinates, and basic game information. The team box score dataset (624 x 57) has information about team performance for every game, including total shots attempted and made, fouls, rebounds, turnovers, and more. It also contains useful information for creating visualizations such as team colors and logos. The player box score dataset (7,140 x 57) contains similar in-game statistics as the team box score dataset but broken down by individual. It also has basic player information including position and whether or not the player is a starter. Across all datasets, very little cleaning was required apart from the removal of the All Star game.

salary_info <- function(url) {
  Sys.sleep(1)
  webpage <- read_html(url)

  team <- webpage %>%
    html_element("h1.salary_title") %>%
    html_text(trim = TRUE)
  
  rows <- webpage %>%
    html_elements("tbody tr")

  map_df(rows, function(row) {

    player <- row %>%
      html_element("td.salary_player_name a") %>%
      html_text(trim = TRUE)

    salary_cell <- row %>%
      html_elements("td.salary_cap_hit") %>%
      .[1]

    salary <- salary_cell %>%
      html_attr("sorttable_customkey") %>%
      as.numeric()

    contract_type <- salary_cell %>%
      html_attr("title") %>%
      na_if("")

    tibble(
      name = player,
      team = team,
      salary = salary,
      contract = contract_type
    )
  })
}

Analysis and Insights

Team Performance

We began our analysis by looking into team performance, identifying the strongest and weakest teams in the league.

Season records

As a starting point, we made a visualization of the progression of each team’s record, represented by cumulative wins, over the course of the season. This provides insight into how team’s performed relative to each other at each point in the season, information that cannot be derived from their records alone. A grey dashed line was added to mark the end of the regular season and beginning of the playoffs. The lines for each team end at varying points, depending on if they made the playoffs and when they were eliminated.

Game Margins

As a way of investigating what factors contribute to the success of dominant teams and downfall of weaker teams, game margins were calculated for various statistics, including field goals, free throws, three pointers, turnovers, and fouls. For example, if a team made two more field goals than their opponent in a game, that would be a margin of +2. If they had one less turnover than their opponent, that would be -1. The averages of these margins across all games were calculated for each team, providing a window into how they typically match up against opponents.

Code 
# determine game margins for different performance stats
margins <- wnba_team_records %>%
  group_by(game_id) %>%
  mutate(
    field_margin = field_goals_made - rev(field_goals_made),
    free_margin = free_throws_made - rev(free_throws_made),
    three_margin = three_point_field_goals_made - rev(three_point_field_goals_made),
    turnover_margin = turnovers - rev(turnovers),
    foul_margin = fouls - rev(fouls)) %>%
  ungroup() %>%
  select(game_id, team_name, field_margin, free_margin, three_margin, 
    turnover_margin, foul_margin, cumulative_wins) %>%
  arrange(game_id)

Predicting Season Outcomes

Machine learning algorithms provide another technique for determining factors that contribute to team success. We built a classification model of game outcome (win or loss) using LASSO, an algorithm which shrinks coefficients toward 0. By kicking out predictors, LASSO helps identify the features that are most important to our model.

We trained our LASSO model on all of the regular season games, and tested it on the playoff games.

Code 
# remove columns that are unnecessary for predicting game outcomes
wnba_team_lasso <- wnba_team_box %>%
  # filter out all star game
  filter(game_id != 401781604) %>%
  select(-season, -game_date_time, -team_id, -team_uid, -team_slug, -team_location, 
      -team_abbreviation, -team_display_name, -team_short_display_name, -team_color, 
      -team_alternate_color, -team_logo, -team_score, -opponent_team_id, 
      -opponent_team_uid, -opponent_team_slug, -opponent_team_location, 
      -opponent_team_abbreviation, -opponent_team_display_name, 
      -opponent_team_short_display_name, -opponent_team_color, 
      -opponent_team_alternate_color, -opponent_team_logo, -opponent_team_score) %>%
  mutate(
    team_winner = factor(team_winner),
    largest_lead = as.numeric(largest_lead),
    fast_break_points = as.numeric(fast_break_points),
    turnover_points = as.numeric(turnover_points)) %>% 
  mutate(season_day = as.numeric(game_date - as.Date("2025-05-16"))) %>%
  select(-game_date)

# create training dataset with all games before playoffs
lasso_training <- wnba_team_lasso %>%
  filter(season_type != 3) %>%
  select(-game_id, -season_type)

# create test dataset with playoffs
lasso_test <- wnba_team_lasso %>%
  filter(season_type == 3) %>%
  select(-game_id, -season_type)

# model specification
set.seed(212)
model_spec <- logistic_reg() %>%
  set_mode("classification") %>% 
  set_engine("glmnet") %>%
  set_args(mixture = 1, penalty = tune())

# variable recipe
data_rec <- recipe(team_winner ~ . , data = lasso_training) %>%
  step_novel(all_nominal_predictors()) %>%
  step_dummy(all_nominal_predictors())

# workflow (algorithm + recipe)
model_wf <- workflow() %>% 
  add_recipe(data_rec) %>%
  add_model(model_spec) 

# estimate 50 LASSO models
multiple_models <- model_wf %>%
 tune_grid(
    grid = grid_regular(
      penalty(range = c(-5, 3)),     
      levels = 50
    ),
    resamples = vfold_cv(lasso_training, v = 10),
    metrics = metric_set(accuracy)
 )

# determine parsimonious parameter
parsimonious_param <- multiple_models %>%   
  select_by_one_std_err(metric = "accuracy", desc(penalty))

# determine best (highest accuracy) parameter
best_param <- multiple_models %>%   
  select_best(metric = "accuracy")

# build final parsimonious model
final_model <- model_wf %>% 
  finalize_workflow(parsimonious_param) %>% 
  fit(data = lasso_training)

# print coefficients
final_model %>%
  tidy() %>%
  filter(estimate != 0)

# get training accuracy
multiple_models %>% 
  collect_metrics() %>%
  filter(penalty == parsimonious_param$penalty)

The predictors used in our final model include assists, blocks, defensive rebounds, field goal percent, flagrant fouls, fouls, free throws made, largest lead, steals, three point field goals made, total rebounds, turnover points, turnovers, Lynx, and Wings.

Fouls and turnovers both had negative coefficients, meaning they decrease the odds of a team winning, which supports our insights from the previous visualization.

Our final model had a lambda value of 0.027 and a cross-validated accuracy of 0.87. This means that our model correctly predicts the game outcome for 86.61% of new games.

Almost all of the outcomes incorrectly predicted by the model were games in which a team with a massive lead ended up losing. This demonstrates that although the variables outlined above are generally strong predictors of team success, upsets can happen.

## Motivation

In 2019, women made up 44% of all sports participants, but received less than 5% of sports media coverage and less than 1% of total sports sponsorship dollars. In recent years, however, women’s sports have experienced a rapid rise in popularity. This has coincided with the growth of the Women’s National Basketball Association (WNBA). The 2025 season set new records for both attendance and viewership. The league, which currently consists of 13 teams, has announced that it plans to expand to 18 teams by 2030. The growth of the WNBA has also created new opportunities for women off the court with more women occupying positions as media commentators, analysts, coaches, and executives. However, there is still work to be done. Although the WNBA is receiving more attention, athletes aren't being compensated at an equivalent rate. Players, uniting under slogans like "Pay Us What You Owe Us," are demanding a larger cut of the league's increased media revenue. As student-athletes, we were interested in exploring the 2025 WNBA season, analyzing performance at the team and individual levels and how it relates to player earnings and popularity.

## Research Question

Our primary research question is: **what factors were associated with individual and team success throughout the 2025 season?**

In this analysis, we present insightful ways of measuring and visualizing team and player performance. We incorporate machine learning for the purposes of determining the best predictors of game outcome and validating our summaries of player performance. We also explore the relationships between player performance and between salary and social media presence.

The averages of these margins across all games were calculated for each team, providing a window into how they typically match up against opponents.

###### Predicting Season Outcomes

Machine learning algorithms provide another technique for determining factors that contribute to team success. We built a classification model of game outcome (win or loss) using LASSO, an algorithm which shrinks coefficients toward 0. By kicking out predictors, LASSO helps identify the features that are most important to our model.

We trained our LASSO model on all of the regular season games, and tested it on the playoff games.

```{r, results='hide'}

  set_engine("glmnet") %>%

# variable recipe

  step_novel(all_nominal_predictors()) %>%
  step_dummy(all_nominal_predictors())

# workflow (algorithm + recipe)

  add_recipe(data_rec) %>%
  add_model(model_spec) 

# estimate 50 LASSO models

 tune_grid(
    grid = grid_regular(
      penalty(range = c(-5, 3)),     
      levels = 50
    ),

    metrics = metric_set(accuracy)
 )

# determine parsimonious parameter

  select_by_one_std_err(metric = "accuracy", desc(penalty))

# determine best (highest accuracy) parameter
best_param <- multiple_models %>%   
  select_best(metric = "accuracy")

# build final parsimonious model

# print coefficients

  filter(estimate != 0)
```
