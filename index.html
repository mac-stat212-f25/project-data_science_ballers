Motivation

In 2019, women made up 44% of all sports participants, but received less than 5% of sports media coverage and less than 1% of total sports sponsorship dollars (Togethxr). In recent years, however, women’s sports have experienced a rapid rise in popularity. This has coincided with the growth of the Women’s National Basketball Association (WNBA). The 2025 season set new records for both attendance and viewership. The league, which currently consists of 13 teams, has announced that it plans to expand to 18 teams by 2030. The growth of the WNBA has also created new opportunities for women off the court with more women occupying positions as media commentators, analysts, coaches, and executives. However, there is still work to be done. Although the WNBA is receiving more attention, athletes aren’t being compensated at an equivalent rate. Players, uniting under slogans like “Pay Us What You Owe Us,” are demanding a larger cut of the league’s increased media revenue. As student-athletes, we were interested in exploring the 2025 WNBA season, analyzing performance at the team and individual levels and how it relates to player earnings and popularity.

Passionate fans of the Golden State Valkyries whose inaugural season was 2025 (Beth LaBerge/KQED).

Research Question

Our primary research question is: what factors were associated with individual and team success throughout the 2025 WNBA season?

In this analysis, we present insightful ways of measuring and visualizing team and player performance. We incorporate machine learning for the purposes of determining the best predictors of game outcome and validating our summaries of player performance. We also explore the relationships between player performance and between salary and social media presence.

Background

The WNBA is the professional women’s basketball league in the United States. There are 13 teams in the WNBA, and there are 12 rostered players (players that are actively playing) on each team. The entire league is split into two conferences, the Eastern and Western Conferences. Every team plays 44 games in a regular season, most games against teams in their conference but some outside. After the regular season ends, the best teams move onto an elimination tournament called the playoffs. The playoffs include the top 8 teams in either conference, based on win-loss record. These teams compete in a series of games where losing teams are gradually eliminated until only one remains. The final winner is crowned the WNBA champion.

Because our project analyzes team and individual performance, it is important to understand the common statistics that help measure performance:

  • Points per game (PPG): total number of points scored per game

  • Shots: total number of shots taken, including missed shots

  • Assists: passes made that directly led to a point scored

  • Blocks: blocking and opponents shot to prevent a point

  • Steals: taking the ball away from the opponent before they can take a shot

  • Rebounds: gaining possession of the ball after a missed shot (offensive or defensive)

  • Turnovers: the opposite of steals; losing the ball to the opponent before they take a shot

  • Record: this is the total amount of wins and losses that a team accumulates

Data

Wehoop is an R package for analyzing women’s college and professional basketball data. It is a scraping and aggregating interface for ESPN’s women’s college basketball and WNBA statistics developed by Saiem Gilani and Geoff Hutchinson. In this analysis, we use wnba play-by-play, team box score, and player box score datasets from 2025. The play-by-play dataset (121,342 x 62) contains every play from every game. It has text explaining the type of play (layup, rebound, foul, substitution, free throw, etc.) and players involved. It also has timestamps, player coordinates, and basic game information. The team box score dataset (624 x 57) has information about team performance for every game, including total shots attempted and made, fouls, rebounds, turnovers, and more. It also contains useful information for creating visualizations such as team colors and logos. The player box score dataset (7,140 x 57) contains similar in-game statistics as the team box score dataset but broken down by individual. It also has basic player information including position and whether or not the player is a starter. Across all datasets, very little cleaning was required apart from the removal of the All Star game.

    html_element("h1.salary_title") %>%
    html_text(trim = TRUE)

    html_elements("tbody tr")

      html_text(trim = TRUE)

      html_attr("title") %>%
      na_if("")

    tibble(
      name = player,
      team = team,
      salary = salary,
      contract = contract_type
    )
  })

Analysis and Insights

Team Performance

We began our analysis by looking into team performance, identifying the strongest and weakest teams in the league.

Season records

As a starting point, we made a visualization of the progression of each team’s record, represented by cumulative wins, over the course of the season. This provides insight into how team’s performed relative to each other at each point in the season, information that cannot be derived from their records alone. A grey dashed line was added to mark the end of the regular season and beginning of the playoffs. The lines for each team end at varying points, depending on if they made the playoffs and when they were eliminated.

Game Margins

As a way of investigating what factors contribute to the success of dominant teams and downfall of weaker teams, game margins were calculated for various statistics, including field goals, free throws, three pointers, turnovers, and fouls. For example, if a team made two more field goals than their opponent in a game, that would be a margin of +2. If they had one less turnover than their opponent, that would be -1. The averages of these margins across all games were calculated for each team, providing a window into how they typically match up against opponents.

Code 
# determine game margins for different performance stats
margins <- wnba_team_records %>%
  group_by(game_id) %>%
  mutate(
    field_margin = field_goals_made - rev(field_goals_made),
    free_margin = free_throws_made - rev(free_throws_made),
    three_margin = three_point_field_goals_made - rev(three_point_field_goals_made),
    turnover_margin = turnovers - rev(turnovers),
    foul_margin = fouls - rev(fouls)) %>%
  ungroup() %>%
  select(game_id, team_name, field_margin, free_margin, three_margin,

Predicting Season Outcomes

Machine learning algorithms provide another technique for determining factors that contribute to team success. We built a classification model of game outcome (win or loss) using LASSO, an algorithm which shrinks coefficients toward 0. By kicking out predictors, LASSO helps identify the features that are most important to our model.

We trained our LASSO model on all of the regular season games, and tested it on the playoff games.

Code 
# remove columns that are unnecessary for predicting game outcomes
wnba_team_lasso <- wnba_team_box %>%
  # filter out all star game
  filter(game_id != 401781604) %>%
  select(-season, -game_date_time, -team_id, -team_uid, -team_slug, -team_location, 
      -team_abbreviation, -team_display_name, -team_short_display_name, -team_color, 
      -team_alternate_color, -team_logo, -team_score, -opponent_team_id, 
      -opponent_team_uid, -opponent_team_slug, -opponent_team_location, 
      -opponent_team_abbreviation, -opponent_team_display_name, 
      -opponent_team_short_display_name, -opponent_team_color, 
      -opponent_team_alternate_color, -opponent_team_logo, -opponent_team_score) %>%
  mutate(
    team_winner = factor(team_winner),
    largest_lead = as.numeric(largest_lead),
    fast_break_points = as.numeric(fast_break_points),
    turnover_points = as.numeric(turnover_points)) %>% 
  mutate(season_day = as.numeric(game_date - as.Date("2025-05-16"))) %>%
  select(-game_date)

# create training dataset with all games before playoffs
lasso_training <- wnba_team_lasso %>%
  filter(season_type != 3) %>%
  select(-game_id, -season_type)

# create test dataset with playoffs
lasso_test <- wnba_team_lasso %>%
  filter(season_type == 3) %>%
  select(-game_id, -season_type)

# model specification
set.seed(212)
model_spec <- logistic_reg() %>%
  set_mode("classification") %>% 
  set_engine("glmnet") %>%
  set_args(mixture = 1, penalty = tune())

# variable recipe
data_rec <- recipe(team_winner ~ . , data = lasso_training) %>%
  step_novel(all_nominal_predictors()) %>%
  step_dummy(all_nominal_predictors())

# workflow (algorithm + recipe)
model_wf <- workflow() %>% 
  add_recipe(data_rec) %>%
  add_model(model_spec) 

# estimate 50 LASSO models
multiple_models <- model_wf %>%
 tune_grid(
    grid = grid_regular(
      penalty(range = c(-5, 3)),     
      levels = 50
    ),
    resamples = vfold_cv(lasso_training, v = 10),
    metrics = metric_set(accuracy)
 )

# determine parsimonious parameter
parsimonious_param <- multiple_models %>%   
  select_by_one_std_err(metric = "accuracy", desc(penalty))

# determine best (highest accuracy) parameter
best_param <- multiple_models %>%   
  select_best(metric = "accuracy")

# build final parsimonious model
final_model <- model_wf %>% 
  finalize_workflow(parsimonious_param) %>% 
  fit(data = lasso_training)

# print coefficients
final_model %>%
  tidy() %>%
  filter(estimate != 0)

# get training accuracy
multiple_models %>% 
  collect_metrics() %>%
  filter(penalty == parsimonious_param$penalty)

Individual Performance

Season outcomes and team performance stem from the performance of individual players. In the next component of our analysis, we developed and validated a metric for determining the best players in the league.

Player Impact Estimate

Player Impact Estimate (PIE) is a performance metric developed by the NBA to evaluate a player’s contribution to their team’s success in a specific basketball game. It includes both offensive and defensive metrics and balance positive impacts against negative impacts. 10 is considered to be a solid PIE score. The equation used to calculate PIE can be seen below.

We calculated PIE for each player in every game and then calculated the season average for each player. The players with the 10 highest season average PIE scores are listed below.

  full_join(game_totals, by = "game_id") %>%
  select("game_id", "game_date", "athlete_id", "athlete_display_name", "team_id",

      "field_goals_attempted", "free_throws_attempted", "defensive_rebounds",
      "offensive_rebounds", "assists", "steals", "blocks", "fouls", "turnovers",
      "total_points", "total_field_goals_made", "total_free_throws_made",
      "total_field_goals_attempted", "total_free_throws_attempted",
      "total_defensive_rebounds", "total_offensive_rebounds", "total_assists",
      "total_steals", "total_blocks", "total_fouls", "total_turnovers") %>%

      total_steals + (total_blocks/2) - total_fouls - total_turnovers)) * 100)

Conclusions

Our exploration of team performance, individual performance, and player earnings and recognition has provided valuable insights into the factors that contributed to the the success of teams and players during the 2025 WNBA season. We found that the abilities of a team to outscore and play cleaner basketball than their opponents are strong determinants of their success. Game outcomes can be predicted with reasonable accuracy based on a combination of positive metrics that increase the likelihood of winning such as shots made, rebounds, blocks, as well as negative metric including turnovers and fouls. The most successful teams also had some of the overall league’s most impactful players, which can be determined using the comprehensive Player Impact Estimate (PIE) metric. We found that the relationship between player performance and earnings and recognition is complicated. Typically, only players who have been in the league for many years experience salary increases with stronger performance. However, these veteran players may actually be making less overall than their younger, less experienced counterparts. Individual brand deals are becoming increasingly more lucrative. Because popularity is a primary factor in securing brand deals, many of the league’s younger players who have developed under the growing spotlight on women’s basketball and benefited from Name, Image, and Likeness (NIL) deals in college, are having better success tracking down endorsements in the WNBA. This discrepancy in earnings underscores the importance of player demands for a larger share of league revenues and more equitable compensation.

Limitations and Future Work

The Wehoops datasets were clean, and they had ample information that captured the complexity of explaining basketball performance. One limitation is that we were only given data for the 2025 season making it difficult to draw general conclusions about what success in basketball really means for teams and across players’ careers. Another limitation is that salary alone does not capture how much a player makes from their career, as brand deals are a common way that players create income. These means of income are often correlated with a player’s popularity. Measuring popularity is also a difficult process on its own. In our report, we do this using the number of Instagram followers a player has, however, this poses another limitation as followers are not the only thing that represents popularity.

In the future, it would be ideal to look at and compare multiple seasons. It also would be interesting to look at what different players’ and teams’ general playing styles are, and how that can impact their success. Factors such as the speed at which a team plays, how often they rotate players in and out, what sort of plays they run, and other factors that are not as easily quantified.

Also, we found that there are many aspects other than performance that can affect popularity and salary, such as the type of contract and overall changes of popularity of the sport at different points in each player’s career. Additionally, the player performance vs. popularity percentile plot revealed some interesting grouping. Most of the players in the bottom right quadrant, who we concluded are receiving more recognition than is “deserved” based on PIE alone, are younger and white. On the other hand, the players in the top left quadrant, who we identified as under-rated based on PIE and followers, are mostly older and black. This drove us to consider a question that went beyond the scope of our current project, but nonetheless is very interesting: Are there any confounding social factors, such as race, conventional beauty standards, controversy, political opinions, and sexuality, that impact popularity and pay? Overall, while our report is a comprehensive review of the 2025 season, measuring performance and success in many aspects, there is so much more to explore with this topic.

## Background

The WNBA is the professional women’s basketball league in the United States.
There are 13 teams in the WNBA, and there are 12 rostered players (players that are actively playing) on each team.
The entire league is split into two conferences, the Eastern and Western Conferences.

After the regular season ends, the best teams move onto an elimination tournament called the playoffs.

These teams compete in a series of games where losing teams are gradually eliminated until only one remains.
The final winner is crowned the WNBA champion.

Because our project analyzes team and individual performance, it is important to understand the common statistics that help measure performance:

-   Points per game (PPG): total number of points scored per game

-   Shots: total number of shots taken, including missed shots

-   Assists: passes made that directly led to a point scored

-   Blocks: blocking and opponents shot to prevent a point

-   Steals: taking the ball away from the opponent before they can take a shot

-   Rebounds: gaining possession of the ball after a missed shot (offensive or defensive)

-   Turnovers: the opposite of steals; losing the ball to the opponent before they take a shot

-   Record: this is the total amount of wins and losses that a team accumulates

###### Game Margins

As a way of investigating what factors contribute to the success of dominant teams and downfall of weaker teams, game margins were calculated for various statistics, including field goals, free throws, three pointers, turnovers, and fouls.
For example, if a team made two more field goals than their opponent in a game, that would be a margin of +2.
If they had one less turnover than their opponent, that would be -1.
The averages of these margins across all games were calculated for each team, providing a window into how they typically match up against opponents.

```{r}
# determine game margins for different performance stats
margins <- wnba_team_records %>%

    foul_margin = fouls - rev(fouls)) %>%
```

###### Predicting Season Outcomes

Machine learning algorithms provide another technique for determining factors that contribute to team success.
We built a classification model of game outcome (win or loss) using LASSO, an algorithm which shrinks coefficients toward 0.
By kicking out predictors, LASSO helps identify the features that are most important to our model.

We trained our LASSO model on all of the regular season games, and tested it on the playoff games.

```{r, results='hide'}
# remove columns that are unnecessary for predicting game outcomes
wnba_team_lasso <- wnba_team_box %>%
  # filter out all star game
  filter(game_id != 401781604) %>%
  select(-season, -game_date_time, -team_id, -team_uid, -team_slug, -team_location, 
      -team_abbreviation, -team_display_name, -team_short_display_name, -team_color, 
      -team_alternate_color, -team_logo, -team_score, -opponent_team_id, 
      -opponent_team_uid, -opponent_team_slug, -opponent_team_location, 
      -opponent_team_abbreviation, -opponent_team_display_name, 
      -opponent_team_short_display_name, -opponent_team_color, 
      -opponent_team_alternate_color, -opponent_team_logo, -opponent_team_score) %>%
  mutate(
    team_winner = factor(team_winner),
    largest_lead = as.numeric(largest_lead),
    fast_break_points = as.numeric(fast_break_points),
    turnover_points = as.numeric(turnover_points)) %>% 
  mutate(season_day = as.numeric(game_date - as.Date("2025-05-16"))) %>%
  select(-game_date)

# create training dataset with all games before playoffs
lasso_training <- wnba_team_lasso %>%
  filter(season_type != 3) %>%
  select(-game_id, -season_type)

# create test dataset with playoffs
lasso_test <- wnba_team_lasso %>%
  filter(season_type == 3) %>%
  select(-game_id, -season_type)

# model specification
set.seed(212)
model_spec <- logistic_reg() %>%
  set_mode("classification") %>% 
  set_engine("glmnet") %>%
  set_args(mixture = 1, penalty = tune())

# variable recipe
data_rec <- recipe(team_winner ~ . , data = lasso_training) %>%
  step_novel(all_nominal_predictors()) %>%
  step_dummy(all_nominal_predictors())

# workflow (algorithm + recipe)
model_wf <- workflow() %>% 
  add_recipe(data_rec) %>%
  add_model(model_spec) 

# estimate 50 LASSO models
multiple_models <- model_wf %>%
 tune_grid(
    grid = grid_regular(
      penalty(range = c(-5, 3)),     
      levels = 50
    ),
    resamples = vfold_cv(lasso_training, v = 10),
    metrics = metric_set(accuracy)
 )

# determine parsimonious parameter
parsimonious_param <- multiple_models %>%   
  select_by_one_std_err(metric = "accuracy", desc(penalty))

# determine best (highest accuracy) parameter
best_param <- multiple_models %>%   
  select_best(metric = "accuracy")

# build final parsimonious model
final_model <- model_wf %>% 
  finalize_workflow(parsimonious_param) %>% 
  fit(data = lasso_training)

# print coefficients
final_model %>%
  tidy() %>%
  filter(estimate != 0)

# get training accuracy
multiple_models %>% 
  collect_metrics() %>%
  filter(penalty == parsimonious_param$penalty)
```

The predictors included in our final model and their coefficients can be seen in the equation below.

![](figures/LASSO_eq.png){fig-align="center"}

Fouls and turnovers both had negative coefficients, meaning they decrease the odds of a team winning, which supports our insights from the previous visualization.

Our final model had a lambda value of 0.027 and a cross-validated accuracy of 0.87.
This means that our model correctly predicts the game outcome for 86.61% of new games.

#### Individual Performance

Season outcomes and team performance stem from the performance of individual players.

###### Player Impact Estimate

Player Impact Estimate (PIE) is a performance metric developed by the NBA to evaluate a player's contribution to their team's success in a specific basketball game.
It includes both offensive and defensive metrics and balance positive impacts against negative impacts.
10 is considered to be a solid PIE score.
The equation used to calculate PIE can be seen below.

![](figures/PIE_eq.png){fig-align="center"}

We calculated PIE for each player in every game and then calculated the season average for each player.
The players with the 10 highest season average PIE scores are listed below.

```{r}
# calculate game totals for PIE metric calculation
game_totals <- wnba_team_box %>%

    total_steals = sum(steals),
    total_blocks = sum(blocks),
    total_fouls = sum(fouls),
    total_turnovers = sum(turnovers)
  ) %>%

player_game_totals <- wnba_player_box %>%
  full_join(game_totals, by = "game_id") %>%
  select("game_id", "game_date", "athlete_id", "athlete_display_name", "team_id", 
      "team_name", "points", "field_goals_made", "free_throws_made", 
      "field_goals_attempted", "free_throws_attempted", "defensive_rebounds",
      "offensive_rebounds", "assists", "steals", "blocks", "fouls", "turnovers",
      "total_points", "total_field_goals_made", "total_free_throws_made",
      "total_field_goals_attempted", "total_free_throws_attempted",
```

###### Principle Component Analysis

As an alternative method of assessing player performance, we used principle component analysis (PCA), a machine learning technique for reducing the dimensionality of data.
It turns a set of correlated features into a smaller set of uncorrelated features, called principal components (PCs), which preserve the majority of information in the data.
This is useful for analysis as we have many in-game stats that could be used to determine player performance.


Several of the players in the bottom right quadrant of the plot with positive hype scores, including Paige Bueckers, Angel Reese, Caitlin Clark, Cameron Brink, and Hailey Van Lith are young players who have played in the WNBA for two or less years.
These athletes were also some of the most popular college players.
Apart from Aliyah Boston, the players with negative hype scores are all veterans who have been in the league since before it began to grow in popularity.
This suggests that where a player's college career falls on the timeline of women's basketball's growth has a greater bearing on their recognition and popularity than their performance in the league.

## Conclusions

Our exploration of team performance, individual performance, and player earnings and recognition has provided valuable insights into the factors that contributed to the the success of teams and players during the 2025 WNBA season.
We found that the abilities of a team to outscore and play cleaner basketball than their opponents are strong determinants of their success.
Game outcomes can be predicted with reasonable accuracy based on a combination of positive metrics that increase the likelihood of winning such as shots made, rebounds, blocks, as well as negative metric including turnovers and fouls.
The most successful teams also had some of the overall league's most impactful players, which can be determined using the comprehensive Player Impact Estimate (PIE) metric.
We found that the relationship between player performance and earnings and recognition is complicated.
Typically, only players who have been in the league for many years experience salary increases with stronger performance.
However, these veteran players may actually be making less overall than their younger, less experienced counterparts.
Individual brand deals are becoming increasingly more lucrative.
Because popularity is a primary factor in securing brand deals, many of the league's younger players who have developed under the growing spotlight on women's basketball and benefited from Name, Image, and Likeness (NIL) deals in college, are having better success tracking down endorsements in the WNBA.
This discrepancy in earnings underscores the importance of player demands for a larger share of league revenues and more equitable compensation.

## Limitations and Future Work

The Wehoops datasets were clean, and they had ample information that captured the complexity of explaining basketball performance.
One limitation is that we were only given data for the 2025 season making it difficult to draw general conclusions about what success in basketball really means for teams and across players’ careers.
Another limitation is that salary alone does not capture how much a player makes from their career, as brand deals are a common way that players create income.
These means of income are often correlated with a player’s popularity.
Measuring popularity is also a difficult process on its own.
In our report, we do this using the number of Instagram followers a player has, however, this poses another limitation as followers are not the only thing that represents popularity.

In the future, it would be ideal to look at and compare multiple seasons.
It also would be interesting to look at what different players’ and teams’ general playing styles are, and how that can impact their success.
Factors such as the speed at which a team plays, how often they rotate players in and out, what sort of plays they run, and other factors that are not as easily quantified.

Also, we found that there are many aspects other than performance that can affect popularity and salary, such as the type of contract and overall changes of popularity of the sport at different points in each player’s career.
Additionally, the player performance vs. popularity percentile plot revealed some interesting grouping.
Most of the players in the bottom right quadrant, who we concluded are receiving more recognition than is “deserved” based on PIE alone, are younger and white.
On the other hand, the players in the top left quadrant, who we identified as under-rated based on PIE and followers, are mostly older and black.
This drove us to consider a question that went beyond the scope of our current project, but nonetheless is very interesting: Are there any confounding social factors, such as race, conventional beauty standards, controversy, political opinions, and sexuality, that impact popularity and pay?
Overall, while our report is a comprehensive review of the 2025 season, measuring performance and success in many aspects, there is so much more to explore with this topic.
